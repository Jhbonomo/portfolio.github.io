Mapping the State of a User Experience

In complex enterprise systems, the "user" isn't always the paying customer, yet their experience has a direct impact on satisfaction, adoption, and ultimately churn. To uncover and communicate this hidden layer, I analyzed the findings across 4 years, focused on mapping how users actually perceived and navigated our product.
2020-2024 4 years UX Researcher

About the research Project

Quorum, a US-based company for Government Affairs, executes annually a usability survey to assess user perception of the system experience. Quorum's annual survey has two parts:

  • The System Usability Scale, an industry standard survey containing 10 questions to evaluate user perceptions of a system experience.
  • A mix of open-ended and rating questions covering overall impressions and feature-specific preferences.

My Contributions

In previous years, I supported the research execution. This year, in my new role as Product Design Manager, I led the entire project, overseeing planning, execution, and analysis across multiple stakeholders, working alongside the design team, product and marketing.

Analyzing the System Usability Scale results

Looking across multiple years of data uncovered a clear story: the same issues kept resurfacing, and usability scores followed consistent patterns. A closer breakdown of the SUS statements highlighted the real culprits: steep learning curves and overwhelming complexity, both persisting as major barriers.

Unnecessary Complexity

Many workflows were more difficult than they needed to be, creating friction and frustration that went unnoticed internally.

Learnability Gap

The product was conceptually challenging, raising barriers for new users and impacting long-term engagement.

Recurring problems across the years

Reviewing past years made one thing clear: the same usability issues kept resurfacing, signaling a pattern of neglect. This led me to reflect on how to better capture the real impact of usability on a product's success.

Reporting on system-wide challenges

In my presentation, I reframed the recurring problems as critical risks rather than minor inconveniences, making their weight harder to ignore. The next challenge, however, goes beyond identification: it's about stepping back and finding ways to measure how usability shapes adoption, retention, and the risk of customer churn.

Additionally, focusing on the system-wide challenges of learnability and complexity, I created a report that mapped the factors driving these issues. To address recurring problems, I conducted a deeper analysis of the specific features driving these difficulties in the Feature assessment.

Feature Assessment findings

While the usability scale provides a bird's-eye view of overall system perception, the second part delivers important insights into poorly-rated dimensions. User comments revealed ongoing pain points that many users consistently experienced.

Organizing the data

The qualitative data is classified in three dimensions:

  • Product feature
  • User sentiment (positive, negative, neutral or ambivalent)
  • UX heuristics (e.g. Visual organization, Error management, Consistency, etc.)

With that I picked the features with the highest usage alongside the popularity at SUS (count of mentions to the feature)

Reported on the top 5 features, including why the feature, what are the key topics and recommendations.

Reporting and supporting strategy

Usability debt board

Drawing from a comprehensive analysis of user feedback and usability metrics, I created a strategic implementation plan that prioritized improvements by balancing impact and feasibility.

To operationalize this, I introduced a usability debt board, a structured backlog of high-value issues categorized by effort levels. The design team was responsible for refining these items, detailing solutions, and validating them with users when necessary.

Each product team with frontend capacity could have a dedicated ‚Äúusability enhancement‚Äù slot. They‚Äôd be free to pick one ticket from the bucket that aligns with their expertise and fits into their schedule without disrupting their roadmap. We could plan the bucket with three levels of effort:

  • Low effort: Quick fixes: text adjustments, improved readability, pure CSS changes
  • Medium effort: UI reorganizations or standardization that don't change core functionality (e.g., a bugged rich text editor)
  • High effort: Changes requiring new product requirements, stakeholder/user validation, or in-depth functional behavior changes (e.g. revamping filtering experience).

Key Insights & Learnings

This long-term research project revealed fundamental truths about how enterprise software users experience and interact with complex systems.

Critical Discoveries

  • Hidden Users Matter: In enterprise systems, the end user's experience directly impacts customer satisfaction and retention
  • Neglect Compounds: Small issues that persist over time become significant barriers to adoption and satisfaction
  • Complexity Has Consequences: Unnecessary complexity creates measurable friction that impacts business outcomes
  • Learnability is Strategic: How easily users can understand and adopt the product affects long-term engagement

Methodological Learnings

  • Longitudinal research reveals patterns that short-term studies miss
  • Combining quantitative and qualitative data provides the most compelling evidence for stakeholders
  • Framing issues as business risks rather than UX problems increases stakeholder engagement
  • Cross-functional recommendations ensure broader organizational buy-in and implementation

Reflection & Value

This project demonstrated that systematic UX research can be a powerful driver of organizational change. By delivering clear, evidence-based insights over time, I was able to bring clarity to a scattered problem that was difficult to piece together and act upon.

The most valuable learning wasn't merely identifying problems, it was uncovering the underlying factors that prevent an organization from taking action.
